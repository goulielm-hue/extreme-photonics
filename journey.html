1995–2002
Crete — Where it began
University of Crete & IESL-FORTH, Heraklion, Greece

BSc in Physics (2000) and MSc in Physics (2002) at the University of Crete. Research assistant at the Institute of Electronic Structure and Laser (IESL) at FORTH from 1999 — working on high-order harmonic generation and the development of kilohertz extreme-ultraviolet light sources. These early years in one of Greece's premier laser laboratories set the direction: ultrafast optics, attosecond phenomena, and the ambition to measure the unmeasurable.

KHz EUV light source Appl. Phys. B 73, 687 (2001)
Attosecond pulse characterisation Appl. Phys. B 74, 197 (2002)
2002–2005
Vienna & Munich — Measuring light waves for the first time
TU Wien & LMU Munich · PhD in Physics (LMU, 2005) · Group of Ferenc Krausz

Doctoral research with Ferenc Krausz, first at TU Wien and then at LMU Munich. During these years, the field of attosecond science was being born — and Eleftherios was at the centre. His PhD work achieved the first direct measurement of the electric field of a light wave, published in Science in 2004. The concept was dubbed "the oscilloscope of light waves" and opened the way to lightwave electronics.

2005–2018
Munich — Building the synthesizer, rewriting the rules
Max Planck Institute of Quantum Optics · Team Leader, then Research Group Leader

Thirteen years at the Max Planck Institute of Quantum Optics produced a cascade of firsts. As team leader and then independent research group leader, Eleftherios conceived and built the light-field synthesizer — the instrument that would become the foundation for all subsequent research. He also served as Coordinator of the Max Planck Centre of Attosecond Science (MPC-AS) and External Lecturer at LMU Munich.

The breakthroughs came in rapid succession: single-cycle nonlinear optics (Science 2008), real-time observation of valence electron motion (Nature 2010), synthesized light transients (Science 2011), the first high-harmonic generation in bulk solids (Nature 2015), the optical attosecond pulse — the shortest controlled event of light ever created at 380 attoseconds (Nature 2016, Guinness World Record) — petahertz electronic metrology (Nature 2016), and soft X-ray excitonics (Science 2017).

During this period three of the four major prizes were awarded: the G. Foteinos Prize from the Academy of Athens (2007), the IUPAP Prize in Optics (2009), and the Gustav Hertz Prize from the DPG (2013).

2010–2016
Pohang — Attosecond science across the Pacific
POSTECH · Adjunct Professor of Physics · MPC-AS partner

In parallel with the Munich years, Eleftherios held an Adjunct Professorship at Pohang University of Science and Technology in South Korea, teaching the graduate course "Attosecond Physics: Tools and Techniques" and co-coordinating the Max Planck Centre of Attosecond Science. He also authored the book Attosecond Photonics (World Scientific, 2016) for graduate courses in ultrafast science.

2018–now
Rostock — Extreme Photonics Labs
W3 Professor of Physics · Institute of Physics · University of Rostock

In August 2018, Eleftherios accepted a W3 Professorship at the University of Rostock, founding the Extreme Photonics Labs as a fully independent research group. New infrastructure was built from scratch, a new team assembled, and the scientific ambition expanded into condensed matter physics.

The results have been transformative. Laser picoscopy was demonstrated in 2020 — the first real-space photographs of valence electrons in crystals. In 2023, attosecond electron pulses of just 53 attoseconds were generated from metallic nanotips, the shortest ever measured. An ERC Advanced Grant (€2.5M) was awarded for ultrafast picoscopy of solids. The PHz spectroscopy programme resolved a decades-long debate about the insulator-to-metal transition in VO₂. And the light-field synthesis platform was extended to Yb-based systems and into the mid-infrared at 2 μm via a DFG-funded OPCPA source.
