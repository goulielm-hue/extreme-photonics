Research Programme

Four directions at the frontier of light and matter

The essential chemical, electronic, and mechanical properties of matter are determined by how valence electrons are distributed among atoms — and how external fields reshape those distributions. X-ray techniques can probe material structure with angstrom resolution but cannot directly visualise valence electrons. Ultrafast lasers can track their dynamics on attosecond time scales but cannot spatially resolve them — at least not in the conventional way. Our central objective is to devise new methodologies in which spatial and temporal resolution co-emerge from intense laser–matter interactions: laser pulses short enough to freeze electronic motion on attosecond time scales, yet intense enough to drive the interaction into the extreme nonlinear regime that unlocks atomic-scale spatial sensitivity. This vision unfolds across four interconnected research directions.

Direction 01 · Enabling Technology

Light-Field Synthesis & Attosecond Metrology

Valence electrons respond most naturally to visible and near-UV light, yet for the first decade of attosecond science this spectral range lacked attosecond resolution. Our group set out to “re-conquer” the attosecond frontier at optical frequencies — to control and confine light fields on time scales shorter than their own oscillation cycle (~2 fs). The answer is the light-field synthesizer: an advanced apparatus that divides a multi-octave coherent spectrum, generated in a gas-filled hollow-core fibre, into several independently controlled spectral bands and recombines them into a single, precisely sculpted waveform.

This technology delivered two broadly recognised breakthroughs. In 2011 we demonstrated the first field-synthesised transients controllable on sub-cycle time scales and used them to induce and measure ultrafast electronic coherences in atoms. In 2016 we achieved the optical attosecond pulse — a waveform confining more than half its energy within a single half-cycle of its field, just 380 attoseconds, the shortest light ever generated. These pulses reach intensities of ~1014 W/cm², opening entirely new possibilities in attosecond nonlinear and extreme nonlinear optics.

The platform is now being extended from Ti:Sapphire to Yb-based laser systems for higher average power, and into the mid-infrared at 2 µm — enabling damage-free extreme photonics in low-bandgap semiconductors, correlated oxides, topological insulators, and quantum materials whose energy gaps were previously inaccessible to waveform-controlled intense fields.

Direction 02 · ERC Advanced Grant

Dynamic Picoscopy — Photographing the Chemical Bond

For more than a century, X-ray and electron diffraction have probed the bulk of condensed matter with atomic resolution — yet they scatter off the total electron density and cannot isolate the valence electrons that govern chemical reactivity, electrical transport, and optical response. Advancing microscopy to the next level calls for a technique that is selectively sensitive to valence electrons and inherently compatible with ultrafast time-resolved methods.

We proposed that, in the strong-field limit, high-harmonic generation in bulk solids can be described as a direct scattering of a laser-driven electron wavepacket off the valence crystal potential. The intensity and angular dependence of emitted harmonics map onto the complex Fourier components (structure factors) of that potential — the diffraction spots on a screen are replaced by high harmonics of the driving pulse. Measuring harmonic yield versus field amplitude and crystal orientation gives direct access to these structure factors, enabling complete real-space reconstruction of the valence electron density at sub-angstrom resolution. We call this technique Laser Picoscopy.

First demonstrated in MgF₂ and CaF₂, picoscopy revealed electron density distributions whose ionic radii differ markedly from neutral-atom values — a direct fingerprint of the chemical bond. Our long-term vision is the complete visualisation of the chemical bond at a level of detail sufficient to predict chemical and electronic properties of materials directly from experiment, without external theoretical models. The ERC Advanced Grant ULPIS (€2.5 M, 2023–2028) now extends picoscopy to three dimensions, from ionic to covalent systems — silicon, SiC, diamond, organic crystals such as glycine — and to real-time movies of electron and atomic motion across quantum phase transitions.

Direction 03 · Nature 2023

Strong-Field Nanooptics — Attosecond Electrons from Nanotips

Optical field emission of electrons from nanostructured materials is a promising route to PHz-scale electronic signal processing and electron-based microscopies with sub-femtosecond temporal and angstrom spatial resolution. At metallic nanotips, geometric field enhancement concentrates the driving laser to strengths rivalling atomic binding fields within a few cubic nanometres — quenching electron quiver motion, creating steep ponderomotive gradients, and confining emission and recollision to sub-cycle time windows far shorter than in the gas phase.

Experiments in our group with intense single-cycle optical pulses on tungsten nanotips (apex radius ~40 nm) have produced electrons with kinetic energies exceeding 150 eV — a key prerequisite for applications such as low-energy electron diffraction and recollision-based structural imaging. To characterise these bursts in time, we developed homodyne attosecond streaking (HAS): the emitted electrons are streaked by a weak replica of the driving field itself, producing spectrograms analogous to conventional attosecond streaking but entirely in the solid state. This technique yielded the first generation and complete temporal characterisation of isolated attosecond electron pulses from a nanostructure — the shortest electron pulse ever measured, at 53 as.

The programme is now advancing toward crystalline nanotips of different metals, exploring multielectron dynamics in the strong-field regime, and developing light-induced electron diffraction for atomic-scale structural imaging at surfaces.

Direction 04 · CRC LiMatI

PHz Spectroscopy of Quantum Phase Transitions

A key discovery of our research in extreme photonics in bulk solids is that high-harmonic radiation from crystals acts as a macroscopic probe of electronic currents whose nature resembles ordinary electronic circuitry — but scaled up in frequency by several thousand times. These multi-PHz light-induced currents open the prospect of advancing signal processing to the petahertz frequency range and, more broadly, of using intense laser fields as a fundamentally new tool for electronic metrology in condensed matter.

Building on this foundation, our PHz phase-resolved spectroscopy uses single-cycle light transients with super-octave bandwidth to identify nascent metallic phases through their intrinsic free-carrier plasma edge — a broadband, model-independent marker that conventional narrow-band probes cannot access. Applied to vanadium dioxide, this framework resolved a decades-long mechanistic debate about the insulator-to-metal transition, revealing that the transition is rate-limited by anharmonic lattice dynamics rather than instantaneous electronic switching.

At the sub-cycle frontier, we combine optical attosecond pulses with soft X-ray attosecond probes to track how core-excited states in solids relax on femtosecond and attosecond time scales — directly accessing dark-state dynamics invisible to conventional near-edge spectroscopy. This attosecond transient-reflectivity approach opens a new class of multi-dimensional experiments with sub-femtosecond precision in Mott-insulating and correlated-electron systems.
