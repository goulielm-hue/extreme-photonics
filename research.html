Research Programme

Four directions at the frontier of light and matter

We develop experimental methods in which atomic spatial resolution and attosecond temporal resolution co-emerge from the interaction of intense, precisely shaped laser fields with matter. The foundation is light-field synthesis — the ability to control multi-octave optical spectra on sub-cycle time scales — deployed across four interconnected research directions.

Direction 01 · Enabling Technology

Light-Field Synthesis & Attosecond Metrology

Probing and manipulating matter on the native time scale of electrons demands control of light fields with sub-cycle precision — temporal resolution finer than their own oscillation period. The light-field synthesizer, developed in our group over more than a decade, divides a multi-octave coherent spectrum into independently controlled spectral channels that are recombined into a single, precisely sculpted waveform.

The platform is now being extended from Ti:Sapphire to Yb-based laser systems for higher average power, and into the mid-infrared at 2 μm — opening extreme nonlinear optics to correlated oxides, topological insulators, and other quantum materials whose bandgaps were previously inaccessible.

Direction 02 · ERC Advanced Grant

Dynamic Picoscopy — Photographing the Chemical Bond

When an intense laser pulse drives a crystalline solid into the extreme nonlinear regime, the emitted high harmonics encode the structure factors of the valence crystal potential. Inverting this relationship yields real-space maps of the valence electron density at sub-angstrom resolution — we call this technique Laser Picoscopy.

Unlike X-ray diffraction, which scatters off the total electron density, picoscopy is selectively sensitive to the valence electrons that govern chemical reactivity, electrical transport, and optical response. The ERC Advanced Grant ULPIS now extends picoscopy to three-dimensional imaging, structurally sensitive measurements under external stimuli, and real-time movies of electron and atomic motion across quantum phase transitions.

Direction 03 · Nature 2023

Strong-Field Nanooptics — Attosecond Electrons from Nanotips

At metallic nanostructures, geometric field enhancement concentrates the driving laser field to strengths comparable to atomic binding fields within a volume of a few cubic nanometres. This introduces qualitatively new physics: the quenching of electron quiver motion, steep ponderomotive gradients, and the confinement of electron emission and recollision to sub-cycle time windows far shorter than in the gas phase.

Using our homochromatic attosecond streaking (HAS) technique, we achieved the first generation and complete temporal characterisation of isolated attosecond electron pulses from a solid-state nanostructure — recovering the shortest electron pulse ever measured. The programme is now advancing toward light-induced electron diffraction from crystalline nanotips, targeting atomic-scale structural imaging at surfaces.

Direction 04 · CRC LiMatI

PHz Spectroscopy of Quantum Phase Transitions

Conventional narrow-band spectroscopies cannot unambiguously identify the onset of a metallic phase — photoexcited carriers in the insulating state mimic genuine metallisation. Our PHz phase-resolved spectroscopy uses single-cycle light transients with super-octave bandwidth to identify the nascent metallic phase through its intrinsic free-carrier plasma edge — a broadband, model-independent marker that narrow-band probes cannot access.

Applied to vanadium dioxide, this framework resolved a decades-long mechanistic debate about the insulator-to-metal transition, revealing that the transition is rate-limited by anharmonic lattice dynamics rather than instantaneous electronic switching. At the sub-cycle frontier, we perform attosecond transient-reflectivity spectroscopy of Mott-insulating systems, accessing a new class of multi-dimensional experiments with sub-femtosecond precision.
